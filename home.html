Title 18: Crimes and Criminal Procedures

This title was enacted in 1948 for all federal crimes and is similar to the set of penal codes found in each individual state. This title covers every crime and process in a multitude of situations. This particular title contains several components which help organize the procedures and crimes including:
    I. Crimes
    II. Criminal Procedures
    III. Prison and Prisoners
    IV. Correction of Youthful Offenders
    V. Immunity of Witnesses
    Each section has many different chapters, subsections, definitions, and amendments. Only a few out of many will be highlighted on this website. To view a specific section, click here.

Part I.: Crimes

Includes crimes in 123 categories in areas from war crimes to child support

Part II.: Criminal Procedure

This section of Title 18 includes all aspects of the proceedings after a committed crime. In 237 categories, this section lays out the trial, forensics, arrest, rights of the criminal, witness protection, etc.; every process following a crime.

Part III.: Prison and Prisoners

Outlines the bureaucracy of prisons as well as logistics regarding prisoners themselves: women's institutions, transfer to or from foreign countries, prisoners with mental health issues, etc.

Part IV.: Correction of Youthful Offenders

Deals with minors having committed crimes and the unique processes followed in that case.

Part V.: Immunity of Witnesses

The general definitions and processes of witness immunities as well as the court and grand jury proceedings, administrative proceedings, and congressional proceedings.

Title 18 Statutes with a Focus on Civil Rights

*not every statute is covered, as there are many hundreds of provisions under this title. This particular set of statutes come from the FBI's website found here.

    Title 18, U.S.C., Section 249
      Hate Crime Protection: this statute essentially lays out the consequences of committing a crime upon another intentionally because of their skin color, religion, ethnicity, gender, disability status, etc. There are processes in place to fund a thorough investigation into if a crime falls into this category. Maximum sentence is 10 years unless the hate crime was committed in conjunction with kidnapping or aggravated sexual assault and/or abuse, in which case the sentence will be extended.
    Title 18, U.S.C., Section 248
      This statute prohibits obstructing, blocking, or in any way making access to a clinic for reproductive health difficult. In addition to physical force, this statute prohibits the use of intimidation or verbal threat of force to any person wishing to enter the clinic. This includes getting services like an abortion, medication (birth control pills, emergency contraception, etc.) Furthermore, harm to the building itself is prohibited and each of the aforementioned offenses can result in fines or jail time depending on the severity of the disruption.
    Title 18, U.S.C., Section 241
      Any group of two or more is prohibited from planning to injure or harm another or otherwise "conspire against their rights". The punishment for this crime includes fines and jail time, and similarly to Section 249, consequences will be greater in conjunction with kidnapping or sexual assault.
    Title 18, U.S.C., Section 14141
      This section prohibits acts by law enforcement such as using excessive force, acting in a discriminatory manner, making a false arrest, inappropriate sexual conduct, etc. This is in reference to an officer of laws actions when dealing with juveniles. This is designed to protect minor's rights by ensuring fair treatment from law enforcement.

    The Importance of Title 18: Who does it protect?

      This title and its contents protect everyone in our country--from children, the elderly, minorities, women, and even endangered animals. It covers many aspects of crimes and criminal procedures pertinent to every situation and is constantly being ammended to include more groups and provide protection in the toughest of circumstances.
